Change and Resiliency:

How the Worst in Life Can Bring Out the Best in You

It’s interesting for me to look back and see how far I’ve come with my level of resiliency over the last ten years since I became an “adult”.  The fact that I experienced so many traumatic experiences that nearly destroyed me early on, is exactly why I became the resilient person I am today.  Recognizing that bad experiences were likely to keep coming throughout the rest of my life (and seeing no indication that they would ever stop), it became a matter of survival: adapt or die.  Not having any natural resiliency strengths to lean on, I learned to develop them over time.

One example of my poor resiliency skills in the past happened when I was 20 years old.  My gram (grandmother) was my best friend since the day I was born and I saw her more as a “mom” than my biological mother. 

She died in a house fire one night in 2007, a night when I was supposed to be there with her and wasn’t.  She was 74 so, obviously, I didn’t expect her to live forever, but I also didn’t expect to lose her so suddenly or in such a horrifically violent manner.  I felt like she deserved more years in the life she loved and a better, more peaceful death when it was her time.  I was angry and couldn’t accept this sudden change.  I lost all sight of rational thought and allowed myself to fall completely down the rabbit hole of destructive thoughts and behavior.

At the time, I had no other support network outside of the very person who I’d just lost.  I was also struggling with depression and a related drug addiction due to other traumatic experiences that had happened in the 5 years prior to that.  I had no positive view of the future or inner direction and I lost any tattered shred of spirituality that I had still been clinging to previously.

My resistance to the change lasted 6 years and I fought HARD to stay in that dark place.  Eventually, though, I got tired of being sad and angry…and stuck.  I started letting people back into my life and rebuilding relationships with others (some positive, some not, but the ratio started leaning more heavily towards positive with time).  I started finding things to be positive about in my life, my future and myself.  Most importantly, I started remembering and honoring my grandmother’s spirit.  Rather than being sad for the loss of her life, I started to live every day as a tribute to her memory.

When I look back at the first 30 years of my life, this event was definitely one of two defining moments for me, and I credit them both with making me the resilient person I am today.  As a result, I am much more confident about my ability to handle setbacks in the future.  I figure that I’ve already experienced a few of the worst setbacks a person can possibly face in life and I survived (even when I thought I couldn’t possibly).  

Anything that gets thrown at me from here on out will either be just as bad or nowhere near as bad…and I know that I can handle it either way.



~Joni-Lynn
