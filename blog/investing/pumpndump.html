Pump and Dump

Sometimes the most expensive "tips" are free

The pump and dump scam is arguably the most common stock market scam out there. It involves a person or group of people (either within the company or outside of it) buying an ultra-cheap stock, then giving an endorsement (usually done through e-mail chains, websites and social media) to a group of unsuspecting potential buyers that they should pick it up immediately. It’s usually offered as a “hot,” insider tip and the next big break for investors (the stocks used in executing this scheme are often referred to as “chop stocks”).

These buyers (read: victims of false advertising) then go buy the suggested stock in large quantities, inflating the price. At this point, the original perpetrators of the scam sell their own stocks making a huge profit for themselves and leaving the unsuspecting buyers holding the bag (and the financial loss) when the stock drops back through the floor.

In recent years, the scam has evolved to a new, more elaborate form: the HACK, pump and dump. This is essentially the same scheme as above but with an element of cybercrime added in for good measure. Scammers buy large amounts of an otherwise worthless penny stock with the intent of artificially inflating its value. Through phishing efforts, hackers gain access to vulnerable brokerage firms’ accounts, where they proceed to buy huge amounts of their chosen penny stock.

This looks like (to people on the outside with no knowledge of what’s happening) a miraculous surge and a deal that they have to get in early on. So, day traders unknowingly buy into the scheme thinking they’ve discovered a golden ticket. The price of the stock goes up even higher, and the original hackers/scammers cash out with a huge payday, leaving the brokerage firms and the day traders who fell for the scheme angry and wondering how they just lost millions of dollars.
